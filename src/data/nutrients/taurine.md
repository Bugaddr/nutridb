name: Taurine
altName: 2-Aminoethanesulfonic acid

category: other
subcategory: conditionally-essential
essentiality: conditionally-essential
sortOrder: 32
description: An amino acid-like compound abundant in heart, brain, and retina. Not incorporated into proteins. Essential in cats (can't synthesize it). Conditionally essential in premature infants.
functions:
  - Bile acid conjugation (fat digestion)
  - Cell membrane stabilization
  - Antioxidant and anti-inflammatory
  - Calcium signaling in heart muscle
  - Retinal development and function
  - Osmoregulation (volume regulation of cells)
  - Central nervous system function (inhibitory neuromodulator)

myths:
  - myth: Taurine in energy drinks comes from bull semen
    reality: This urban legend is false. Taurine in energy drinks is synthetic. The name comes from "taurus" (bull) because it was first isolated from ox bile in 1827, not from reproductive organs.
  - myth: Taurine in energy drinks is dangerous
    reality: Taurine itself is very safe. Any health concerns from energy drinks are primarily from caffeine, sugar, and combined effects.
precautions:
  - Cat food MUST contain taurine — cats that eat dog food or non-supplemented diets develop fatal heart disease and blindness
  - Energy drinks typically contain 500-1000 mg taurine — well within safe limits
interactions:
  - substance: Caffeine
    effect: Taurine may modulate some of caffeine's excitatory effects
  - substance: Antihypertensive medications
    effect: Taurine has mild blood pressure-lowering effects — may be additive
history: Taurine was first isolated from ox bile in 1827 by Friedrich Tiedemann and Leopold Gmelin, hence the name from Latin "taurus" (bull). Its biological importance became clear in the 1970s when taurine-deficient cats developed retinal degeneration and cardiomyopathy. A 2023 Science study found taurine supplementation extended lifespan in mice by 10-12%.
