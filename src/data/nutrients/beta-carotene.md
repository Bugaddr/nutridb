myths:
  - myth: Beta-carotene supplements prevent cancer
    reality: The ATBC and CARET trials showed that beta-carotene supplements INCREASED lung cancer risk in smokers by 18-28%. This was one of the most shocking findings in nutrition research. Food sources remain protective.
  - myth: You can get vitamin A toxicity from eating too many carrots
    reality: The body only converts beta-carotene to vitamin A as needed. Excess beta-carotene from food causes harmless orange skin but NEVER vitamin A toxicity.
precautions:
  - SMOKERS AND ASBESTOS-EXPOSED INDIVIDUALS SHOULD NOT TAKE BETA-CAROTENE SUPPLEMENTS
  - Beta-carotene from food is safe and beneficial — the risk is from supplements
  - Conversion efficiency varies 3-28x between individuals based on genetics (BCMO1 gene)
  - Cooking and fat improve absorption vs raw vegetables
interactions:
  - substance: Smoking
    effect: High-dose supplements paradoxically become pro-oxidant in the oxidative environment of smokers' lungs
  - substance: Fat
    effect: Required for absorption — minimum 3-5g with the meal
  - substance: Lutein
    effect: May compete for absorption at supplemental doses
history: Beta-carotene was isolated in 1831 by Heinrich Wilhelm Ferdinand Wackenroder from carrots (carota = carrot). The conversion to vitamin A was demonstrated in the 1930s. The ATBC (1994) and CARET (1996) trials showing increased cancer risk in smokers taking supplements were landmark studies that altered the supplement industry and our understanding of antioxidants.
funFacts:
  - The name "carotene" comes from "carota" (carrot in Latin) — carrots get their orange color from beta-carotene
  - Flamingos, salmon, and lobsters get their color from carotenoids closely related to beta-carotene
  - If you eat enough carrots, your skin will literally turn orange — this is called carotenodermia and is harmless
  - The body's conversion of beta-carotene to vitamin A has a built-in safety mechanism — it downregulates as stores fill up
