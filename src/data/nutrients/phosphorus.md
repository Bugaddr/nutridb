description: Second most abundant mineral in the body after calcium. Critical for bone structure, energy metabolism (ATP), and DNA/RNA backbone.
functions:
  - Structural component of bones and teeth (with calcium as hydroxyapatite)
  - Component of ATP, ADP — cellular energy currency
  - Structural backbone of DNA and RNA (phosphodiester bonds)
  - Part of cell membranes (phospholipids)
  - Acid-base buffering in blood

myths:
  - myth: Phosphorus deficiency is common
    reality: The opposite — most people get too much phosphorus from processed foods, soft drinks, and food additives. Deficiency is extremely rare.
precautions:
  - Processed foods contain phosphate additives that are nearly 100% absorbed vs ~50% from natural sources
  - High phosphorus intake may worsen kidney disease
  - Cola drinks are high in phosphoric acid — excessive consumption may harm bone health
interactions:
  - substance: Calcium
    effect: High phosphorus can reduce calcium absorption and vice versa
  - substance: Aluminum-containing antacids
    effect: Bind phosphorus in the gut, reducing absorption
history: Phosphorus was discovered in 1669 by Hennig Brand, who isolated it from urine — making it the first element discovered by a known individual. The name means "light bearer" from its glow. Its biological importance was recognized in the 19th century.
funFacts:
  - Phosphorus was first isolated from urine — Brand boiled 1,500 gallons of urine to get it
  - Every cell in your body contains phosphorus
  - Your DNA backbone is literally made of phosphorus atoms linking nucleotides together
  - Phosphorus glows in the dark (white phosphorus) — hence the name "light bearer"
