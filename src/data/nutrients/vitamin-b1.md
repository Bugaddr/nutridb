myths:
  - myth: Thiamine deficiency is only a problem in developing countries
    reality: Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome from alcohol-related thiamine deficiency is a significant concern in developed nations. Up to 80% of alcoholics have some degree of thiamine deficiency.
  - myth: B vitamins give you energy directly
    reality: B vitamins are coenzymes in energy metabolism — they help convert food to ATP but don't provide energy themselves like calories do.
precautions:
  - Raw freshwater fish and shellfish contain thiaminase enzymes that destroy thiamine
  - Tea and coffee contain anti-thiamine factors
  - 'Thiamine is destroyed by heat, especially in alkaline conditions'
interactions:
  - substance: Alcohol
    effect: Impairs absorption and increases excretion of thiamine
  - substance: Diuretics (furosemide)
    effect: Increase urinary thiamine loss
  - substance: Fluorouracil (chemotherapy)
    effect: May inhibit thiamine phosphorylation
history: 'Beriberi was endemic in Asia where polished white rice was the dietary staple. In 1897, Christiaan Eijkman demonstrated that rice bran cured beriberi in chickens — he won the 1929 Nobel Prize. Casimir Funk coined the term "vitamine" in 1912 while studying the anti-beriberi factor.'
funFacts:
  - The word "vitamine" was originally coined specifically for thiamine — Funk believed it was a vital amine
  - 'Beriberi means "I can''t, I can''t" in Sinhalese — referring to the difficulty of walking'
  - Thiamine triphosphate acts as a neurotransmitter activator independent of its coenzyme role
  - Japanese navy eliminated beriberi in the 1880s by adding barley to the sailors' rice diet
enzymeRoles:
