---
name: Molybdenum
altName: Mo
symbol: Mo
category: mineral
subcategory: trace
essentiality: essential
sortOrder: 27
description: Ultra-trace mineral cofactor for enzymes that metabolize sulfur-containing amino acids and purines. Deficiency is almost unheard of in humans.
functions:
  - Cofactor for xanthine oxidase (purine metabolism)
  - Cofactor for sulfite oxidase (sulfur amino acid metabolism)
  - Cofactor for aldehyde oxidase (drug metabolism)
rda:
  nih:
    - group: Adults
      amount: 45 mcg
    - group: Pregnant/Lactating
      amount: 50 mcg
  who:
    - group: Adults
      amount: 25 mcg
  efsa:
    - group: Adults
      amount: 65 mcg
deficiency:
  symptoms:
    - Rapid heart rate, rapid breathing
    - Night blindness, mental confusion
    - Only documented in ONE case — a TPN patient
  conditions:
    - Sulfite oxidase deficiency (genetic — devastating neurological disease)
  riskGroups:
    - Only TPN patients receiving no molybdenum
toxicity:
  symptoms:
    - Gout-like symptoms (elevated uric acid from xanthine oxidase activity)
    - Joint pain
  upperLimit: 2000 mcg/day for adults
  notes: Occupational inhalation of molybdenum dust has caused gout-like syndrome in workers.
normalLevels:
  bloodRange: 0.1-3.0 ng/mL
  unit: ng/mL
testing:
  methods:
    - Serum molybdenum (rarely tested)
    - Urinary sulfite (would be elevated in deficiency)
  sampleType: Blood/Urine
sources:
  - name: Black-eyed peas (½ cup)
    amount: 288
    unit: mcg
  - name: Lima beans (½ cup)
    amount: 104
    unit: mcg
  - name: Oats (1 cup, cooked)
    amount: 68
    unit: mcg
  - name: Potato (1 medium)
    amount: 36
    unit: mcg
myths:
  - myth: You need to worry about molybdenum intake
    reality: Natural deficiency has never been documented in free-living humans. If you eat any variety of food, you get enough.
precautions:
  - High doses may interfere with copper metabolism
  - Legumes are the richest source — a single serving of lentils exceeds the daily need
interactions:
  - substance: Copper
    effect: Very high molybdenum can interfere with copper metabolism (seen in cattle — "teart" pastures)
history: Molybdenum was discovered as an element in 1778 by Carl Wilhelm Scheele. Its biological role was established in the 1950s when molybdenum cofactor (Moco) was identified in xanthine oxidase. The only documented human dietary deficiency was in a TPN patient in 1981.
funFacts:
  - Only ONE case of dietary molybdenum deficiency has ever been documented in a human
  - The word comes from Greek "molybdos" (lead) because it was confused with lead ore
  - Cattle are far more sensitive to molybdenum imbalance than humans — excess causes "teart" disease
