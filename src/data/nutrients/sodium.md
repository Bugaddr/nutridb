myths:
  - myth: Sea salt is healthier than table salt
    reality: Sea salt contains the same amount of sodium as table salt by weight. The trace minerals in sea salt are negligible. Table salt is usually fortified with iodine, which is actually beneficial.
  - myth: Athletes should drink as much water as possible during exercise
    reality: Excessive water without electrolytes causes exercise-associated hyponatremia — a potentially fatal condition. Athletes should drink to thirst and include sodium.
precautions:
  - Reduce gradually — taste buds adapt in 2-3 weeks
  - Read labels — bread, cereals, and canned foods are major hidden sodium sources
  - Potassium-rich diets help counteract sodium's blood pressure effects (DASH diet)
interactions:
  - substance: Lithium
    effect: Sodium depletion increases lithium levels — dangerous toxicity risk
  - substance: Diuretics
    effect: Can cause hyponatremia by increasing sodium excretion
history: Salt has been one of the most valued commodities throughout history — Roman soldiers were partly paid in salt ("salary" from Latin "salarium"). Salt taxes triggered revolutions (French Revolution, Gandhi's Salt March). The link between salt and hypertension was established in the mid-20th century.
funFacts:
  - The word "salary" comes from the Latin "salarium" — salt money for Roman soldiers
  - Gandhi's 1930 Salt March was a pivotal moment in Indian independence
  - Your body contains ~100g of sodium — about 40% of table salt by weight
  - Only ~11% of dietary sodium comes from salt added at the table — 77% comes from processed/restaurant food
