---
name: Selenium
altName: Se
symbol: Se
category: mineral
subcategory: trace
essentiality: essential
sortOrder: 22
description: A trace mineral essential for thyroid function, antioxidant defense (glutathione peroxidase), and immune health. Brazil nuts are the richest food source.
functions:
  - Component of selenoproteins (25 in humans)
  - Glutathione peroxidase — major antioxidant enzyme
  - Required for thyroid hormone metabolism (iodothyronine deiodinases)
  - Supports immune function and reproduction
rda:
  nih:
    - group: Adults
      amount: 55 mcg
    - group: Pregnant
      amount: 60 mcg
    - group: Lactating
      amount: 70 mcg
  who:
    - group: Adults
      amount: 26-34 mcg
  efsa:
    - group: Adults
      amount: 70 mcg
deficiency:
  symptoms:
    early:
      - "Fatigue"
      - "Muscle weakness"
      - "Brain fog"
      - "Increased susceptibility to illness"
    moderate:
      - "Hair loss"
      - "White nail beds"
      - "Hypothyroid symptoms (T4→T3 conversion impaired)"
      - "Recurrent infections"
      - "Infertility (male — sperm motility)"
    severe:
      - "Keshan disease (cardiomyopathy — endemic in selenium-poor regions of China)"
      - "Kashin-Beck disease (osteoarthropathy)"
      - "Severe myopathy"
      - "Myxedematous cretinism"
    longTerm:
      - "Chronic cardiomyopathy"
      - "Joint degeneration"
      - "Increased cancer risk (prostate, lung, colorectal)"
      - "Chronic thyroid dysfunction"
  conditions:
    - Keshan disease (cardiomyopathy in selenium-deficient regions of China)
    - Kashin-Beck disease (osteoarthropathy)
    - Myxedematous cretinism (with concurrent iodine deficiency)
  riskGroups:
    - People in selenium-poor soil regions (parts of China, Europe)
    - HIV patients
    - Dialysis patients
    - People with GI disorders
toxicity:
  symptoms:
    - Garlic breath odor, metallic taste
    - Hair loss, brittle nails
    - GI disturbances, fatigue
    - Peripheral neuropathy
  upperLimit: 400 mcg/day for adults
  notes: Just 1-2 Brazil nuts per day can provide the entire RDA. Eating handfuls daily can cause selenosis.
normalLevels:
  bloodRange: 70-150 ng/mL
  unit: ng/mL
testing:
  methods:
    - Serum/plasma selenium
    - Selenoprotein P level
    - Glutathione peroxidase activity
  sampleType: Blood (serum/plasma)

myths:
  - myth: Selenium supplements prevent cancer
    reality: The SELECT trial found selenium supplements did NOT reduce cancer risk and may increase type 2 diabetes risk. Early promising results from the NPC trial have not been replicated.
precautions:
  - Just 1-2 Brazil nuts daily meets the RDA — don't eat handfuls
  - Soil selenium content varies dramatically by region, affecting food content
  - Selenium and iodine deficiency together worsen thyroid function more than either alone
interactions:
  - substance: Vitamin C (high doses)
    effect: May reduce selenium absorption from selenite form
  - substance: Heavy metals (mercury, arsenic)
    effect: Selenium may provide some protection against mercury toxicity through binding
history: Selenium was discovered in 1817 by Jöns Jacob Berzelius. It was considered purely toxic until 1957 when Klaus Schwarz showed it prevented liver necrosis in rats. The Keshan disease studies in China in the 1960s-70s proved its essentiality in humans.
funFacts:
  - A single Brazil nut contains more selenium than any other food — one nut exceeds the daily requirement
  - Selenium was named after Selene, the Greek goddess of the moon
  - Selenium-containing soil varies so dramatically that crops from different regions can have 100x different selenium levels
enzymeRoles:
  - "Component of glutathione peroxidases (GPx1-6)"
  - "Component of thioredoxin reductases (TrxR)"
  - "Component of iodothyronine deiodinases (DIO1-3)"
  - "Component of selenophosphate synthetase (SPS2)"
hormonalRoles:
  - "Required for T4 → T3 conversion (thyroid hormone activation)"
  - "Required for T3 → T2 inactivation"
  - "Protects thyroid from oxidative damage during hormone synthesis"

  - "Thioredoxin system (redox regulation)"
