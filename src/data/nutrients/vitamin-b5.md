name: Vitamin B5

category: vitamin
subcategory: water-soluble
essentiality: essential

description: Component of Coenzyme A (CoA), critical for fatty acid synthesis and oxidation, and the citric acid cycle. Found in virtually all foods.
functions:
  - Component of Coenzyme A — central to all energy metabolism
  - Required for fatty acid synthesis and oxidation
  - Needed for synthesis of cholesterol, steroid hormones, and neurotransmitters
  - Supports wound healing and skin health

deficiency:
  symptoms:
    - Extremely rare — "pantothenic" means "from everywhere"
    - Fatigue, headache, irritability
    - Numbness/burning in hands and feet (burning feet syndrome)
    - Sleep disturbances
  conditions:
    - Burning feet syndrome (historically seen in WWII prisoners of war)
  riskGroups:
    - Severe malnutrition only
toxicity:
  symptoms:
    - Diarrhea at very high doses (10+ g/day)
  upperLimit: No established UL
  notes: One of the safest vitamins. Very high doses may cause GI upset.

myths:
  - myth: You need B5 supplements for acne
    reality: While some studies suggest mega-dose pantothenic acid (5-10g/day) may reduce acne, the evidence is limited and such high doses are unnecessary for most people.
precautions:
  - Food processing reduces B5 content by 20-70%
  - Freezing and canning significantly reduce pantothenic acid levels
interactions:
  - substance: Biotin
    effect: B5 and biotin may compete for absorption at high supplemental doses
history: Discovered in 1931 by Roger J. Williams. Named from Greek "pantos" meaning "everywhere" because it's found in nearly all foods. Williams later became a major proponent of nutritional individuality, arguing that every person has unique nutritional needs.
funFacts:
  - The name means "from everywhere" — deficiency is almost impossible on a normal diet
  - Royal jelly (from honeybees) is one of the richest natural sources of B5
  - Dexpanthenol (provitamin B5) is widely used in skin care products and wound healing ointments
