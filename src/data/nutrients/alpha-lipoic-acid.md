description: A sulfur-containing compound that functions as a cofactor in energy metabolism and as a unique antioxidant — active in both water and fat environments.
functions:
  - Cofactor for mitochondrial enzyme complexes (pyruvate dehydrogenase, alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase)
  - Universal antioxidant (works in both aqueous and lipid environments)
  - Regenerates other antioxidants (vitamins C, E, glutathione)
  - Chelates heavy metals
  - Improves insulin sensitivity

myths:
  - myth: ALA is a powerful weight loss supplement

precautions:
  - Can lower blood sugar — diabetics should monitor closely

  - Take on empty stomach for best absorption
interactions:
  - substance: Insulin and diabetes medications

  - substance: Thyroid medications
    effect: May lower thyroid hormone levels — monitor if supplementing
  - substance: Chemotherapy
    effect: Antioxidant effects may theoretically interfere (unproven)
history: Alpha-lipoic acid was discovered in 1951 by Lester Reed, who identified it as a cofactor for pyruvate dehydrogenase. Its antioxidant properties and potential therapeutic uses have been studied since the 1990s. It's an approved drug for diabetic neuropathy in Germany.
funFacts:
  - ALA is the only antioxidant that works in both water and fat — earning it the name "universal antioxidant"
  - It can regenerate other antioxidants (vitamin C, E, glutathione, CoQ10) — a recycler of recyclers
  - Germany has approved ALA as a prescription drug for diabetic neuropathy since the 1960s
